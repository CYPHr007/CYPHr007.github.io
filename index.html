Γ´HIYAL

From predictive maintenance to maintenance intelligence.

My first business started as PILAR, became NOUS, and is now HIYAL. The question that started it stayed the same: if machines show signs before failure, why are those signs still missed? The answer has grown. HIYAL is no longer just a model that predicts. It is becoming the memory layer for a plant, every recommendation, every decision, and every outcome, recorded, and built for any industrial machine.

The name comes from the Arabic ḥiyal (حِيَل), ingenious devices — the classical name for the science of machines, from the Banū Mūsā to al-Jazarī. Engineers who did not just build machines, but wrote them down so the understanding would outlive them. The full story is here.

The system matters to me because it is not abstract. It connects software, machine signals, failure patterns, and the people who actually need to decide whether something is becoming dangerous. Confidence that is earned, not assumed, even when that means the system saying it does not know yet. During the pilot, HIYAL is a free download for anyone who wants to judge it on their own machines.

Δ´Human Archive

A short node, still on the map.

Human Archive gets its own section even though I was only there about a month. In that time it pushed me further into hardware and motion capture, and into the idea that model quality starts much earlier, at the level of how data is physically collected.

Human Archive builds hardware rigs for full-body motion capture, recording how people actually move so that data can be used to train robots. Around it, the work became more physical: rigs, motion, synchronization, and what it takes to collect data that is actually good enough to matter downstream.

I moved on after about a month, back to my own company, but the shift in how I think about data collection, that quality starts at the rig and the sensor, not in the model, stayed with me.

ΣΤ´Infinity

The race in the margin, resolved.

If you scrolled this far, you watched it happen: the tortoise ahead, Achilles behind, the gap halving and halving and never closing. Zeno of Elea posed this about 2,400 years ago as an argument that motion itself is impossible. To catch the tortoise, Achilles must first cover half the remaining distance. Then half of what remains. Then half of that. Infinitely many steps — and nobody, said Zeno, finishes infinitely many things.

½ + ¼ + ⅛ + 1⁄16 + 1⁄32 + ⋯ = 1
infinitely many steps · a finite distance

The resolution is that infinitely many and infinitely much are different things. The steps never end, but they shrink fast enough that they add up to something finite — exactly one, not approximately. That is what infinity means here: not a place at the end of the list, but the direction the list points. The sum never arrives at 1 after some final step; 1 is simply the only number the partial sums crowd against. It took mathematics about two thousand years to say that cleanly — the limit — and calculus, and with it most of engineering, stands on that one idea.

I keep the race in the margin because it is quietly the job description: watching a sequence of small signals converge on an event, and calling it before it arrives.
