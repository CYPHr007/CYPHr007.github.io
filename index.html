My journey

From El Jadida
to Shenzhen.

Morocco

The gamer years

Growing up in El Jadida, I had zero connection to tech. Gaming was my whole universe. I graduated from a French high school without knowing what a line of code looked like. But gaming taught me something: the patience to grind through hard problems, and the obsession to get better.

Summit of Mount Toubkal, 4,167m — age 16.
The Seed

Internship at Kapachim Maroc

Before I knew anything about code, I interned at Kapachim Maroc, a chemical manufacturing plant. I saw the factory floor up close: the machines, the breakdowns, the cost of unplanned downtime. I didn't know it yet, but this experience planted the seed that would become PILAR.

Kapachim Maroc — where the idea for PILAR was born.
The Leap

Leaving home at 18

At 18, I left my parents and everything I knew in Morocco to move to Shenzhen, alone. No safety net. No connections. Barely any Mandarin. I enrolled in Computer Science and Engineering at CUHK Shenzhen. The distance from family was the hardest part — but I knew that if I wanted to build something real, I had to put myself where things are built.

First day in Shenzhen — a new chapter.
The Brotherhood

The people who changed everything

I moved into a small dorm room with 3 other students. They became my brothers. And then there's Rayan — the friend who told me to apply to CUHK Shenzhen. He introduced me to tech, connected me with people in the field, and opened my eyes to the startup ecosystem. I wouldn't be here without him.

With Rayan — the one who started it all.
The Transformation

Zero to everything in under a year

In less than a year, I went from someone who had never coded to someone building full-stack apps, training ML models, and writing AI agents. Python, web development, machine learning, desktop packaging, system design. When you have nothing to fall back on, you learn fast.

Now

Shipping, studying, scaling.

PILAR is live — a desktop app with local AI agents, offline-first, zero cloud dependency. I'm still a CSE student at CUHK Shenzhen, still learning Chinese, still building. The journey from a gamer in Morocco to a founder in China happened in under a year. It's only the beginning.

PILAR — predictive industrial
maintenance.

01

ML-Powered Predictions

GradientBoosting trained on 220k+ real sensor readings. Recall 100%, F1 100%. Detection window up to 5 hours before failure.

02

Local AI Agents

Three specialized agents: diagnostic, maintenance, alert. Powered by Qwen3 via Ollama, running entirely on your machine.

Native Windows installer. Built with PyInstaller and Inno Setup. Lives in your system tray, works without a browser.

Current work

Y Combinator W26 —

YC W26 · Shenzhen

Building the hardware that teaches
robots how to move like humans.

At Human Archive, we're building a full-body multimodal motion capture system from scratch — the hardware, the capture pipeline, the data. The goal: produce the highest-quality human movement datasets ever collected, at scale, to train the next generation of robots.

I'm on the hardware side. That means designing and assembling the physical capture rigs in Shenzhen — the city where this kind of thing is actually possible. Sensors, suits, synchronization, calibration. Every detail matters because the robots learning from this data will depend on it.

Raj Patel, CEO of Human Archive, and Shlock, head of hardware, opened their doors to me. They trusted me with real responsibility at 18 — before a track record, before credentials. That kind of bet changes you. I don't take it lightly.

Most AI labs think about data as a software problem. We think about it as a hardware problem. The bottleneck isn't compute — it's the quality of what you feed the model. We're fixing that.
